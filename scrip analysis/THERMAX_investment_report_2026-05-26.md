## 1. Macro, Sector & Stock Cycle Context

**Broad market (Step 1):** India's broader market is in an Optimistic phase — the Nifty 50 is near YTD highs, mid-cap indices have run 20–30% over the past year, and the capital goods sector is a consensus overweight theme. IPO market remains active. Sentiment is not euphoric but is clearly beyond neutral. Sizing implication: do not concentrate; treat any entry as a moderate-conviction position, not a table-pounding one.

**Sector cycle (Step 1b) — TAILWIND:** India's capital goods sector is forecast to grow 12–14% in FY27 (Crisil, May 2026). Government capital outlay rises to ₹12.2 lakh crore in FY27 (+9% vs FY26). Power now accounts for ~50% of capital goods demand (up from 32%), driven by 58–62 GW capacity additions led by renewables. Industrial private capex is actively recovering in chemicals, pharma, FMCG, metals, and oil & gas. Data centres and EV infrastructure are emerging demand pools. Industry order books total ~₹5.2 lakh crore with a 3.7x book-to-bill ratio (vs 3.1x in FY24). **Primary tailwind driver: structural India power and industrial capex cycle — expected duration 4–6 years.** Only notable headwind is raw material cost volatility; geopolitical risk is limited.

**Stock cycle position (Step 1c) — Late Capex / Early Revenue Ramp:** Full-year FY26 revenue grew only +3% (₹10,389 Cr → ₹10,694 Cr) due to project timing and weak H1, masking a strong Q4 FY26 exit rate of ₹3,428 Cr (annualized ~₹13,700 Cr). The order book of ₹13,604 Cr (27% YoY growth, ~1.3x FY26 revenue) provides ~15 months of revenue visibility. Q4 FY26 order intake was ₹4,490 Cr — 112% YoY — including a landmark ₹1,600 Cr supercritical boiler order via TBWES. Operating margins inflected from 7% in FY21–22 to 10.9% in Q4 FY26. Capex peaked in FY25 at ₹1,220 Cr; FY26 capex moderated to ₹587 Cr, with CWIP of ₹1,392 Cr suggesting capacity under commissioning. **Assessment: Revenue ramp is imminent and credible. This warrants a slightly higher DCF growth assumption than 10-year history alone implies, but also demands a rigorous valuation check since the market has already anticipated it.**

**Industry structure (Step 2):**

**Industry assessment: Mixed-Favorable.** The combination of low substitution threat and medium-low entry barriers gives Thermax a structurally adequate competitive position, though rivalry in large EPC projects (power, refineries) keeps ROICs range-bound.

## 2. Business Quality

Thermax's competitive advantage is **narrow but durable** — built on 50+ years of installed base and customer relationships in industrial process heating, cooling, and air pollution control. The TBWES joint venture with Babcock & Wilcox (USA) is the most significant differentiator: the ₹1,600 Cr supercritical boiler contract won in Q4 FY26 is Thermax's first in this category, opening a high-margin segment previously dominated by BHEL and Chinese OEMs. Four segments — Industrial Products (~51% of revenue), Industrial Infra (~41%), Green Solutions (~7%), Chemicals (~8%) — provide diversification but also complexity.

Management quality is above average. CEO Ashish Bhandari (ex-GE) has brought execution discipline and transparent concall communication since FY21. The Pudumjee family (62% promoter, zero pledging, RDA Holdings 53.99%) is a long-term value creator, not a capital extractor. Capital allocation is sensible: the company deleveraged to near-zero debt in FY20 during the prior downturn, and the current leverage increase (₹2,353 Cr borrowings, D/E 0.42x) is growth-capex-driven, not M&A-driven.

**The structural weakness** is the Green Solutions business — 250 MW operational capacity with significant cost overruns in FY26 due to contractor performance failures. Until this segment demonstrates sustained profitability, it remains a drag on consolidated margins and a source of earnings volatility.

**Moat verdict: Narrow | Holding** — not eroding (ROCE stable at 15% across cycle), not strengthening (no systematic improvement in ROCE toward 18–20% despite revenue doubling since FY21).

*(Sources: Screener.in consolidated 10-year data, accessed May 25, 2026; MarketsMojo Q4 FY26 results analysis)*

**10-year read:** Four important findings emerge that recent-year snapshots obscure.

1. **Full-cycle ROIC barely clears CoC.** Average ROCE across FY17–26 is ~13.1%, and including FY15–16 peaks brings it to ~14.6%. India WACC is ~13–14% (10-yr G-sec 7% + ERP 6% × beta 1.2). Thermax creates value, but the margin above CoC is thin — consistent with a narrow moat, not a wide one. The pandemic trough (FY20–21 ROCE 10–12%) confirms the business is cyclical; ROCE can dip below CoC for 2+ years during downturns.

2. **Revenue stagnation FY15–21 was real.** Revenue went from ₹5,304 Cr in FY15 to ₹4,791 Cr in FY21 — six years of zero growth. The current "17% 5-year CAGR" is partly a recovery from a depressed base. The underlying secular growth rate (10-year) is 8% CAGR, significantly below what the current P/E implies.

3. **Margin structure unchanged.** Operating margins have stayed in a 7–10% band for a full decade with no structural improvement. The FY24–26 improvement to 9–10% is partially cycle-driven and partially product mix. The inability to escape this band despite revenue doubling is the most direct evidence that Thermax lacks pricing power — a narrow moat indicator.

4. **The current capex cycle is legitimate.** FY25 capex ₹1,220 Cr + CWIP ₹1,392 Cr is the largest capacity investment in the company's recent history. Unlike FY15 borrowings that funded working capital, the current leverage funds tangible fixed asset creation (TBWES supercritical boiler facility, green solutions capacity, Exactspace digital platform). Once commissioned (FY27–28), this should drive the next earnings leg — but it is already priced in at 78x TTM P/E.

**FY26 OCF ₹542 Cr vs NP ₹720 Cr (75%) warrants one-quarter of monitoring.** FY25 saw the inverse (OCF ₹1,043 Cr vs NP ₹627 Cr = 166%), suggesting timing-related lumpiness rather than structural earnings inflation. Still, the ₹329 Cr "other income" line (46% of FY26 PAT) is almost entirely investment/interest income on Thermax's cash hoard. Core operational PAT margin is ~5.1% (₹541 Cr / ₹10,694 Cr), not the reported 6.7%.

## 4. Earnings Quality

**Overall assessment: Clean, with two caveats.**

Receivables days improved from 83 to 75 over FY24–26, running counter to the revenue ramp — a positive signal that collections discipline is intact. Inventory days stable at 45–60 range. Promoter pledging: zero (BSE/NSE disclosures). No auditor qualifications flagged in available disclosures.

**Caveat 1 — Other income inflation:** ₹329 Cr in FY26 (46% of PAT) is structurally high. This is investment/interest income on the cash pile and is real income, but it is not operational and should be stripped when valuing the core business. At FY25's OCF-to-NP ratio of 166%, the quality signal was excellent; FY26's 75% is a step-down worth watching in Q1 FY27.

**Caveat 2 — Green solutions losses:** Management acknowledged cost overruns in the FirstEnergy and green solutions segment due to contractor underperformance in FY26. If these persist, they become a margin drag and a source of earnings manipulation risk (capitalizing vs expensing project losses). One quarter of clarity is needed.

**Red flag status: None triggered.** No receivables creep, no policy changes, no auditor issues.

## 5. Valuation

### 5a. Price-Implied Expectations (Mauboussin)

*Read before building a DCF.*

| PIE Value Driver | Market-implied assumption | Base-case assumption | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue CAGR (5-yr, FY27–31) | ~18–20% | 14% | Market optimistic by 4–6 pp |
| NOPAT margin (steady-state, ops) | ~10–11% | 7.5–8% | Market optimistic by 250–350 bps |
| Investment rate (capex + WC / ΔRev) | ~30% | 35–40% | Market assumes capital-lighter growth than likely |

**Calculation methodology:** EV ≈ ₹52,077 Cr; NOPAT FY26 (ops) ≈ ₹600 Cr; WACC = 13.5%; terminal growth 5%. Simple perpetuity back-solve yields ~13% perpetuity NOPAT growth — above India's long-run nominal GDP. Two-stage reverse DCF (10-year explicit + terminal) implies ~45–50% NOPAT CAGR over the explicit period for FCF to discount to ₹52,077 Cr — which is only achievable through simultaneous 18–20% revenue CAGR AND 10–11% NOPAT margin (vs current 5.6%), both sustained for a decade. *(Source: computed from Screener.in financial data and current market metrics)*

**Expectations assessment:** The market is meaningfully too optimistic on both revenue growth and margin expansion. The single driver where the market is *most* wrong: margin. Thermax's 10-year OPM band (7–10%) does not support the 10–11% NOPAT margin that current pricing assumes. Even with Green Solutions scaling and product mix improvement, a 10-year average above 9% NOPAT would be unprecedented for this business.

**Expectations treadmill:** Revenue CAGR must exceed 16% AND core NOPAT margin must sustainably expand above 9.5% — and both simultaneously — for the stock to outperform its own PIE. Good execution at 14% revenue + 8% margins merely earns cost of capital.

**Positive expectation triggers:**
1. Q1 FY27 revenue ≥ ₹2,800 Cr (confirming order book conversion at pace), especially with EBITDA margin ≥ 11%
2. Green solutions segment turns EBIT-positive — removes the margin drag and de-risks the bull case
3. Second large supercritical boiler order win (demonstrates the ₹1,600 Cr award was not one-off)

**Negative expectation triggers:**
1. Q1 FY27 revenue < ₹2,400 Cr or margins below 9.5% — signals execution lag in order-to-revenue conversion
2. FII holdings fall below 9% (further smart-money exit signals valuation ceiling)
3. Raw material cost spike compressing margins on fixed-price contracts (acknowledged as risk in Q4 FY26 call)

**Shareholder Value at Risk (SVAR):** Stress scenario — 8% revenue CAGR FY27–31, NOPAT margins flat at 5.6%, P/E compresses to 35x on disappointing earnings.
FY31E PAT: ₹10,694 Cr × 1.08⁵ × 6.7% = ₹15,705 × 6.7% = ₹1,052 Cr
FY31 market cap at 35x = ₹36,820 Cr → per share ≈ ₹3,100
**SVAR: −30% from current price. Asymmetry is Unfavorable** — limited upside capture unless execution significantly beats PIE, but meaningful downside on any disappointment.

### 5b. Intrinsic Value

**DCF base case (₹1,900/share):** Revenue CAGR 14% for 5 years, fade to 8% for years 6–10, terminal 5%; NOPAT margin expands from 5.6% to 8% by FY31; WACC 13.5%; investment rate 35%. Discount all FCFs to today. EV ≈ ₹20,000–22,000 Cr. Add net cash ₹777 Cr. Equity value ₹21,000 Cr ÷ 11.9 Cr shares ≈ **₹1,900/share** (vs PIE-implied 14% NOPAT CAGR).

At 30× (narrow moat, moderate growth): EV ₹20,400 Cr + net cash ₹2,000 Cr = equity ₹22,400 Cr ÷ 11.9 Cr shares ≈ **₹1,880/share**. At 35×: **₹2,200/share**.

## 8. Key Risks

- **Valuation de-rating risk (HIGH):** At 78x TTM P/E, even modest EPS misses can trigger 25–35% corrections. Cummins India trades at 63x with superior ROCE (~25–30%); Thermax's premium to Cummins is not fundamentally justified.
- **Green Solutions execution risk (MEDIUM):** Contractor failures caused cost overruns in FY26. The 250 MW operational capacity is small relative to the capex deployed. If Green Solutions continues to drag consolidated margins, the bull thesis on margin expansion fails.
- **Fixed-price contract margin risk (MEDIUM):** The ₹1,600 Cr supercritical boiler order is fixed-price over a multi-year delivery period. Unexpected steel/copper price spikes compress margins with no pass-through mechanism.
- **FII selling overhang (MEDIUM):** Foreign holdings fell from 16.02% to 11.24% between June 2025 and March 2026 — a 4.78 pp reduction in 9 months. If this reflects a valuation ceiling, further selling will cap re-rating potential.
- **Capex return risk (MEDIUM):** ₹1,392 Cr in CWIP must translate to revenue within 18–24 months to justify the leverage increase. Delayed project commissioning would hurt FCF and raise net debt further.
