## 1. Macro, Sector & Stock Cycle Context

**Broad market (Step 1):** The Nifty 50 is trading near its upper historical band on a 3-year view, and midcap/smallcap indices have seen significant re-rating. Market sentiment is cautiously optimistic — not euphoric — but small-cap EPC stocks specifically attracted heavy retail flows in FY25–26 on the infrastructure theme, pushing valuations into premium territory. Sizing implication: reduce position size even for a genuine setup; full-size entry is only warranted in fearful market conditions.

**Sector cycle (Step 1b): Tailwind (structural), with near-term caution.** India's T&D sector is backed by an estimated ₹9 trillion capex push through 2032, driven by renewable integration, grid upgrades, and PGCIL's own FY27 capex guidance of ₹37,000 Cr. However, FY26 sector-level ordering slowed sharply — 16 schemes awarded vs. 45 in FY25 — due to utility bandwidth constraints (not structural demand destruction). Input costs (copper, aluminium, steel) are broadly stable. Peer concall commentary from KEC and Kalpataru confirms T&D ordering pipeline remains strong for FY27 but acknowledges near-term execution bottlenecks. Primary tailwind is structural; near-term ordering cadence is weaker. Implication for DCF: do not embed FY26's spike-level revenue growth as the structural baseline; use a faded 25–30% CAGR.

**Stock cycle position (Step 1c): Early cycle / Ramp-up — but inflated.** Revenue went from ₹66 Cr (FY24) to ₹531 Cr (FY26) in two years — a genuine acceleration. However, approximately ₹257 Cr (48%) of FY26 revenue was recognised in March 2026 alone, a single month. The underlying quarterly run-rate (excluding March) is closer to ₹90–100 Cr/quarter, implying an annualised normalised base of ₹360–400 Cr. The company's order book (₹950 Cr confirmed, ₹1,240 Cr active bidding pipeline) provides 1.8–2.3× revenue cover on the normalised base, supporting continued strong growth. New segments (BESS, transformer manufacturing) add optionality. The ramp is real but the Q4 concentration inflates the FY26 reference base, creating mean-reversion risk.

**Industry structure (Step 2):**

| Five Force | Score | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Threat of new entrants | Medium | Low capital needs but requires execution track record and government empanelment |
| Supplier power | Medium | Transformers and cable supply can be tight (lead times extended); Aarsh acquisition partially internalises this |
| Buyer power | **High** | Government utilities (GUVNL, GETCO, DISCOMs) procure via L1 competitive bidding; zero pricing power for contractors |
| Threat of substitutes | Low | No substitute for physical T&D infrastructure |
| Competitive rivalry | **High** | Fragmented: L&T, KEC, Kalpataru at the large end; dozens of regional EPC players at the mid-tier |

**Assessment: Mixed industry structure.** Demand is structurally strong (driven by government mandate), but buyer power is high (L1 procurement), rivalry is intense, and barriers to entry are moderate. Only the best-run, lowest-cost EPC operators sustain attractive ROCE over a full cycle. Viviana at ₹531 Cr revenue is still micro-scale vs. KEC (₹18,000 Cr revenue).

## 2. Business Quality

**Moat: None.** Viviana is a pure-play EPC contractor serving state utilities in Gujarat — primarily GUVNL and GETCO. EPC contractors in India have no sustainable moat: contracts are won through L1 bidding, relationships are useful but not protective, and the sector is fragmented. Revenue was ₹33 Cr as recently as FY22. The company incorporated in 2014; it is barely a decade old.

Two diversification bets are in-flight: (1) **Aarsh Transformers** — 75% stake acquired in a transformer manufacturing subsidiary, expanding to 7 MVA by FY26 and targeting 20.5 MVA by FY27, with a ₹100 Cr greenfield facility near Vadodara approved; (2) **BESS** (Battery Energy Storage Systems) — secured contracts in Rajasthan and Gujarat, contributing ~₹400 Cr to the order book. Both moves are strategically sound for the sector but are unproven at this scale, carry execution risk, and have yet to demonstrate returns on capital.

**Management:** Promoter Nikesh Kishorchandra Chokski holds 66.61%; pledging is 0% (clean). Guidance accuracy is limited to 2 years of public history: Feb 2025 concall guided ₹160–180 Cr revenue for FY25, achieved ₹219 Cr (beat). The company's capital allocation track record is short — but the ₹100 Cr capex for transformer manufacturing plus ₹20 Cr NCDs at 12% suggest willingness to lever up for growth. Whether this capex generates ROIC above cost of capital remains to be seen. **Customer concentration risk is high** — GUVNL and GETCO account for the majority of revenue (Gujarat-state utility dependence).

## 4. Earnings Quality

**FAILS — Concerning.** Three specific red flags trigger the Schilit gate:

1. **Operating cash flow negative against ₹53 Cr PAT.** The cash conversion gap is too large to dismiss as normal EPC working capital. Implied OCF gap = ₹53 Cr profit plus minimal D&A, minus OCF negative = a cash bleed of ₹55–70 Cr on a ₹116 Cr equity base. The company funded this via ₹100 Cr borrowings and ₹20 Cr NCDs at 12%.

2. **Debtor days 267.** Implied trade receivables ≈ ₹388 Cr on ₹531 Cr revenue — **62% of annual revenue is sitting in receivables**. Government DISCOM/TRANSCO payment cycles are notoriously slow in India (UDAY scheme arrears, RDSS implementation delays), but 267 days is in the danger zone even by that standard. KEC International's debtor days are ~100–120 days; Kalpataru runs ~130 days.

3. **48% of FY26 revenue in a single month (March 2026).** EPC seasonality in India (fiscal year-end March 31 push) is real but ₹257 Cr out of ₹531 Cr in one month is extreme. This pattern warrants scrutiny: does it reflect genuine project milestones, or acceleration of billing? Without auditor commentary and individual project-level data, this cannot be confirmed. The Q4 FY26 audited results were signed off, but the sheer quantum is unusual.

**Positives:** Zero promoter pledging, no qualified auditor opinion noted in available disclosures, and 10% dividend recommendation suggests some management confidence in real earnings.

**Conclusion:** Earnings are **Aggressive but Unverifiable.** The framework requires "Concerning or worse → STOP." Given the combination of the three flags above, the earnings quality gate is flagged as Concerning. Downgrade trigger: if receivables remain above 200 days in the H1 FY27 balance sheet.

## 5. Valuation

### 5a. Price-Implied Expectations (Mauboussin)

*Read the price before building a model.*

**Inputs:**
- EV ≈ ₹1,054 Cr (market cap ₹987 Cr + debt ₹100 Cr − investments ₹21 Cr − cash ~₹12 Cr)
- NOPAT = EBIT × (1 − tax) ≈ ₹75 Cr × 0.75 = ₹56 Cr (using FY26 EBITDA; D&A negligible on ₹3 Cr fixed assets)
- WACC = 15% (7% risk-free rate + 1.2 beta × 7% ERP; small-cap EPC premium applied)

**PIE growth (perpetuity approximation):**
g = (EV × WACC − NOPAT) / (EV + NOPAT) = (1054 × 0.15 − 56) / (1054 + 56) = 101.9 / 1110 = **9.2%**

| PIE Value Driver | Market-implied | Your base-case | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue CAGR (5-yr) | ~30% (embedded in 9.2% NOPAT growth) | 25% on ₹450 Cr normalised base | Modest optimism |
| NOPAT margin (steady-state) | ~10–11% | 8–9% (adjusting for cash earnings, not reported) | Negative gap — market too optimistic |
| Investment rate (capex+WC / ΔRevenue) | Low (implied capital-light) | **High** — 267-day debtors mean each ₹1 of revenue consumes ~₹0.73 in receivables financing | Large negative gap |

**Assessment:** The market is pricing in a capital-light, cash-generative business. The actual business is capital-intensive on a working-capital basis — every ₹100 Cr of revenue growth requires ~₹73 Cr of additional receivable financing. This is the single biggest gap between PIE and reality. The market is not wrong on the revenue trajectory; it is wrong on the cash conversion.

**Expectations treadmill:** For the stock to outperform its own PIE, working capital intensity must fall (debtor days to <150 within 2 years) AND NOPAT margins must expand above 11% — simultaneously. Either alone is insufficient.

**Expectation triggers (positive):**
- H1 FY27 balance sheet shows debtor days normalising to <180 (visible in quarterly results)
- BESS segment recognises revenue at better margins (15–18% EBITDA vs EPC's 12–14%)
- Transformer manufacturing (Aarsh) begins contributing revenue at gross margins >25%

**Expectation triggers (negative):**
- H1 FY27 debtor days remain >240 or receivable write-off disclosed
- Q4 FY27 repeats extreme March concentration → confirms a structural booking pattern
- ₹100 Cr capex overshoot or transformer facility delayed beyond FY28

**SVAR:** At downside scenario (20% revenue CAGR on normalised base, 8% NOPAT margin), intrinsic value ≈ ₹320–380. **SVAR = 61–67% from current price.** Asymmetry is **Unfavorable** — significant downside, limited upside beyond current price.

## 8. Key Risks

1. **Earnings quality / receivables impairment**: ₹388 Cr of receivables from state DISCOMs. If even 20% becomes NPA (state utilities have a poor payment track record: UDAY scheme, RDSS delays), equity (₹116 Cr) is wiped out. This is the dominant binary risk.

2. **March-concentration pattern**: If FY27 repeats 40–50% Q4 revenue concentration, it signals a structural billing practice rather than genuine revenue distribution — increasing the risk of audit scrutiny or regulatory questions.

3. **Capex overrun / transformer facility delay**: ₹100 Cr greenfield capacity near Vadodara is funded by high-cost NCDs (12% coupon). If the facility is delayed or misses utilisation targets, interest costs erode the thin net margins.

4. **Competitive pressure from larger EPC players**: As order sizes grow (BESS contracts are ₹400–2,000+ Cr), Viviana will bid against KEC, Kalpataru, and L&T — who have scale, lower costs, and stronger balance sheets.

5. **Zero institutional oversight**: No FII, DII, or mutual fund holding means governance signals (related-party transactions, minority dilution) will not be flagged by institutional monitors. The SME-to-mainboard transition will increase regulatory scrutiny, which is a positive catalyst but also a risk if governance issues surface under the spotlight.
