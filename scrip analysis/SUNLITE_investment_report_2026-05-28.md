## Verdict

**CHEAP BUT FLAWED**

The stock is optically cheap at 16.65× FY26 earnings — below the sector average P/E of 24× — but is built on a no-moat commodity business with dangerously thin margins (1.45%), negative operating cash flow despite ₹40 Cr profit, and an imminent competitive threat from Hindalco's ₹2,000 Cr Gujarat plant. The FY26 revenue doubling was partly inorganic (SAPL acquisition) and partly LME-copper-price-driven pass-through — neither of which is durable alpha. Until OCF turns sustainably positive and the Hindalco plant impact is visible in industry pricing, this is a speculative position only.

## 1. Macro, Sector & Stock Cycle Context

**Broad market (Step 1):** India's Nifty 50 is in a cautious-neutral phase as of May 2026 — the mid-cap and SME segments saw a massive re-rating through FY25 followed by selective corrective pressure. SME-platform stocks, specifically, carry a 5% daily circuit filter that amplifies volatility in both directions. Sentiment is neither euphoric nor fearful; the sizing implication is conservative positioning for SME names. VIX India is moderate.

**Sector cycle (Step 1b): Tailwind — structural, partly cyclical**
India's copper recycling sector is in a genuine structural tailwind. Copper was classified as a Critical Mineral in 2024, strengthening regulatory support. Secondary copper's share of domestic supply is projected to rise from 39% (FY24) to 55% by 2030, driven by EV adoption, renewable energy installations, and power infrastructure expansion. However, a meaningful portion of FY26's revenue growth reflects the LME copper price rally (copper hit multi-year highs in 2024-25); this is cyclical revenue inflation, not structural margin expansion. FY27 volumes may hold, but revenue could normalize if LME copper prices correct 15-20%. Input cost (copper scrap) and output price (copper rod/wire) move roughly in tandem, protecting the processing spread — but this also means revenue size is misleading as a profitability proxy. **Net: Structural demand tailwind is real; cyclical revenue inflation is not.**

**Stock cycle position (Step 1c): Post-injection, trajectory unclear**
Sunlite is in an ambiguous phase post-SAPL acquisition. FY26 showed a 98% revenue and 181% PAT jump — but this is not repeatable organically. Stripping the SAPL contribution and LME-price tailwind, the underlying copper rod/wire business likely grew 15-20% in volume terms. Capacity utilization was 87.76% in FY25 (copper rods at 92.89% — essentially full). FY26 post-SAPL: overall capacity appears to have roughly doubled, creating headroom for the next 2-3 years of volume growth without new greenfield capex. New products ATC (Annealed Tinned Coated Copper) and Copper Busbar add value-added mix. This is early-to-mid cycle for the expanded entity — but the integration risk of SAPL (₹50 Cr deal, mix of cash + equity swap) and the lack of disclosed segment-level profitability for aluminium vs. copper makes the forward earnings profile uncertain.

**Industry structure (Step 2):**

**Overall: Unattractive industry structure.** The only positive is the irreplaceability of copper — everything else works against small-scale processors.

## 2. Business Quality

Sunlite is a secondary copper converter: it buys copper scrap, melts and refines it, and sells copper rods, wires, earthing strips, and conductors to the electrical industry. Founded as a partnership firm in 2017 in Kheda, Gujarat, converted to a limited company in 2022, and listed on NSE SME in August 2024. It employs 38 people — the revenue-per-employee ratio of ~₹73 Cr reveals the pass-through nature of the business (copper metal price dominates revenue, not labour).

**Moat: None.** There is no cost advantage (scrap prices are market-determined), no brand (products are sold to specification), no switching cost (a cable manufacturer can switch copper rod supplier without friction), and no network effect. The Gujarat location near Mundra port is a minor logistical convenience but not a durable advantage.

**Management (Nitin Kumar Heda, promoter-managed):** The Heda family holds 74.47% (no pledging, clean direct structure). The SAPL acquisition (100% of Sunlite Aluminium Private Limited) was executed partly via equity (₹43.5 Cr shares issued) — this is a meaningful dilution for a company with a ₹669 Cr market cap. The rationale (aluminium value chain expansion) is directionally sound for diversification, but the acquisition was related-party adjacent (both entities share the "Sunlite" brand and Gujarat operations), which warrants scrutiny on pricing fairness. Management is aspirational — ATC launch, busbar entry, SAPL integration — but execution track record spans only 3-4 years of audited history.

**Capital allocation:** The SAPL acquisition via mix of cash and equity swap rather than pure FCF signals the company does not yet generate sufficient free cash to fund its growth ambitions internally. Maiden dividend of ₹1/share was declared for FY26 — positive signal of confidence, but the ₹1.4 Cr total dividend cost is trivial relative to the ₹669 Cr market cap.

**Moat durability: N/A** — no moat to be durable. ROCE of 46% is high but is a function of extreme asset turnover (revenue/CE = ~20×) on very thin margins, not pricing power.

**What the 4-year dataset reveals:**

1. **Steadily expanding but wafer-thin margins.** EBITDA has moved from 1.26% to 2.17% — improvement, but the absolute level makes any input cost shock or pricing pressure potentially devastating. A 0.5% adverse move in the processing spread wipes out one-third of profit.

2. **FY26 is an inflection year — but for the right or wrong reasons?** Revenue nearly doubled. Of the ~₹1,368 Cr jump: SAPL acquisition explains a large portion (aluminium scrap + rod volumes); LME copper price elevation explains much of the remainder. 3. **The OCF alarm in FY26.** Operating cash flow turned negative (-₹2 Cr) while PAT was ₹40 Cr. Total assets jumped from ₹94.4 Cr to ₹224.2 Cr — a ₹130 Cr increase — funded by the SAPL share swap (₹43.5 Cr equity), preferential allotment (₹17 Cr), and working capital expansion. For a commodity business that turns its receivables quickly, persistent negative OCF is a structural concern, not just a growth-phase anomaly.

4. **ROCE appears high but is an artefact of asset-light structure, not pricing power.** With revenue of ₹2,765 Cr on capital employed of ~₹130-150 Cr, the asset turnover of ~18-20× means even 0.2% NOPAT margin generates high ROCE. This is mathematically impressive but economically fragile — the margin is the thin line between value creation and destruction.

5. **Increasing dilution.** IPO shares (Aug 2024), preferential allotment (FY26), SAPL swap shares — cumulative share count has risen ~27% from FY25 to FY26. EPS growth is thus overstated relative to per-share cash flow growth.

## 4. Earnings Quality

**Assessment: Aggressive but Defensible — requires monitoring.**

The primary concern is the OCF/PAT divergence in FY26: PAT ₹40.15 Cr, OCF -₹2 Cr. CFO/Operating Profit for FY26 is approximately 20% — well below the threshold of quality earnings (>80%). Three possible explanations:
- **(a) SAPL integration working capital buildup** (defensible): Acquiring a business brings in its receivables and inventory without immediately generating collection cash flow. A one-quarter lag is normal.
- **(b) Revenue-driven WC expansion** (defensible in the short term): 98% revenue growth naturally consumes working capital in copper trading/processing.
- **(c) Aggressive receivables recognition** (concerning): Booking revenue before cash collection to hit growth targets.

The annual report mentions "increased trade receivables and inventory" explicitly. Without access to debtor days and inventory days for FY26, it is not possible to determine whether the CCC has deteriorated. **The OCF situation must be resolved positively in FY27 H1 results for any position to be justified.** Promoter pledging: none detected (clean). Auditor: No qualified opinions flagged in available sources. Related party transactions: SAPL was partly promoter-linked — the pricing of the ₹50 Cr acquisition should be verified against independent valuation.

## 5. Valuation

### 5a. Price-Implied Expectations (Mauboussin)

*Read the price before the model.*

- **EV** = ₹669 Cr (market cap) + ₹12 Cr (debt) ≈ ₹681 Cr
- **NOPAT FY26** ≈ EBIT × (1−t) = ₹56 Cr × 0.746 = **₹42 Cr**
- **WACC**: 14% (risk-free 7.1% + beta ~1.2 × ERP 6.5% = 14.9%; rounded to 14% given low debt)
- **PIE perpetuity**: g = (681 × 0.14 − 42) / (681 + 42) = 53.3 / 723 = **7.4% perpetual NOPAT growth**

| PIE Value Driver | Market-implied | My base case | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue CAGR (5-yr) | ~8–9% (on FY26 elevated base) | 6–8% | Roughly aligned |
| NOPAT margin (steady-state) | ~1.5–1.7% | 1.5–2.0% | Marginally positive (bull) |
| Investment rate (capex+WC / ΔRevenue) | Low (~5–10%) | 20–30% (WC-intensive) | NEGATIVE — market underestimates WC drag |

**Expectations assessment:** The market's 7.4% perpetual growth assumption from the FY26 base is the crux. If FY26 earnings are a clean, repeatable baseline, the stock is cheap. But the negative OCF flags that FY26 NOPAT is not yet converting to cash — the market may be underestimating how much working capital this business will consume as it scales. The most important gap is the investment rate: copper trading/processing businesses typically require 20-30 paise of working capital per rupee of incremental revenue, not the near-zero assumption embedded in the current PIE.

**Expectations treadmill:** For SUNLITE to outperform its PIE, it must simultaneously grow NOPAT at >7.4% AND improve OCF/PAT to >1× — i.e., demonstrate that profits are actual cash, not accounting. Either condition alone is insufficient; both are needed.

**Positive expectation triggers:**
1. FY27 Q1/Q2 results showing OCF strongly positive (>₹15 Cr), confirming WC normalization
2. ATC production ramp generating higher-margin revenue (2.5%+ net margin vs current 1.45%)
3. Government mandates or anti-dumping duty on imported copper rods strengthening domestic pricing

**Negative expectation triggers:**
1. Hindalco plant commissioning in late 2026 → structural pricing pressure on copper rods
2. Receivables or inventory days continue expanding in FY27 → confirms aggressive recognition concern
3. LME copper prices falling 15%+ → revenue compression with margin squeeze on scrap inventory held

**Shareholder Value at Risk (SVAR):** Downside scenario: 5% revenue growth, NOPAT margin at 1.2%, P/E de-rates to 10× → EPS ~₹22 → price ~₹220. SVAR = (484 − 220) / 484 = **54.5% downside**. Asymmetry is Unfavorable.

## 8. Key Risks

1. **Hindalco (Aditya Birla) greenfield threat**: A 300,000–350,000 MT/year integrated copper scrap + e-waste recycling plant in Gujarat is expected to commission by end-2026. Sunlite's entire FY26 revenue is ₹2,765 Cr (~100,000+ MT equivalent). When Hindalco's plant operates at 50% capacity, it will supply more copper product than Sunlite's entire output. This is not a marginal competitor — it is a structural re-ordering of the domestic secondary copper market.

2. **Earnings quality / working capital trap**: If OCF does not normalize to at least 0.5× PAT in FY27, it signals either aggressive revenue recognition or a business that consumes more cash than it earns — value-destroying even at thin margins. The SVAR of 54% captures this downside.

3. **LME copper price cyclicality**: ~98% of revenue is copper metal price pass-through. A 20% decline in LME copper prices compresses revenue by ~₹550 Cr. While processing spread is theoretically price-independent, scrap inventory held during a price decline generates mark-to-market losses that compress near-term profits.

4. **SME platform illiquidity and governance opacity**: With FII ownership at 0% and DII at 0.84%, there is virtually no external analytical or governance check on the company. The 5% daily circuit filter creates asymmetric exit risk in adverse scenarios. Related-party concerns around the SAPL acquisition pricing deserve external scrutiny.

5. **Dilution risk**: The company has already issued shares via IPO (Aug 2024), preferential allotment (FY26), and SAPL swap (FY26). Total dilution from FY25 to FY26 was ~27%. If growth capex or further acquisitions require equity funding (FCF is negative), EPS growth will continue to be partially offset by dilution.
