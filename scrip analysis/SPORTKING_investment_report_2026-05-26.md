## 1. Macro, Sector & Stock Cycle Context

### Broad market (Step 1)

Nifty 50 at ~23,955 on May 25, 2026 — approximately 9% below its all-time high of ~26,277 (September 2024). India VIX at 16.7, declining from elevated early-CY2025 levels. Market is neutral/cautious — Howard Marks's middle zone. No sizing amplifier; position size should be conservative, particularly for cyclical commodity stocks.

### Sector cycle (Step 1b)

**Verdict: HEADWIND. Primary driver is US tariff compression on export economics, structural cotton supply tightness, and near-zero spinning spreads — with nascent FTA tailwinds partially offsetting.**

*End-market demand*: India's textile exports grew just 2.1% in FY26 to ₹3.16 lakh Cr, a deceleration from prior years. Cotton yarn, fabrics and made-ups grew only 0.4%. Man-made yarn at 3.6% was better but not a Sportking strength. Critically, the US — India's largest export market — imposed a reciprocal tariff that peaked at 50% by August 2025 and remained at that level until early February 2026. With exports at ~49–52% of Sportking's revenue, this headwind was direct and severe.

*Input cost cycle*: Cotton prices have been trading at or below the government's Minimum Support Price (MSP) since November 2024 — a meaningful tailwind for yarn spinners. The cost-to-yarn spread has improved from near zero to ₹12–18/kg as of 2026. However, India's cotton output for CY2026 is projected to fall to a decadal low of 29.2 million bales (per ICRA), creating a forward supply tightness risk that could re-erode margins if demand recovers faster than supply.

*Policy calendar (mixed)*: Positive: India–UK FTA (July 2025) eliminated tariffs up to 12% on textile exports to the UK; India–EU FTA (early 2026) opened European markets with near-zero duties. These are genuine multi-year tailwinds for export diversification. Negative: RoDTEP rate cuts for cotton yarn exports have squeezed export margins further. US tariff situation as of FY26 end: still elevated but partially resolved after February 2026.

*Peer operating metrics*: Spinning sector broadly reporting 3–5% operating margins; larger mills (Vardhman, RSWM) with diversification are faring better than pure spinners. Capacity utilisation across the sector is high (~90–95%), limiting near-term pricing power upside.

*Geopolitical / FX*: ~49% of revenue is export (USD/EUR denominated). At ~11% OPM, a 5% INR appreciation would reduce NOPAT by ~20%. No formal hedging policy publicly disclosed. Cotton imports from the US face reciprocal geopolitical exposure.

**Net assessment**: Structural tailwind from FTAs (UK, EU) is real but 2–3 year horizon. Current environment is a HEADWIND: US tariffs, thin spreads, declining cotton crop outlook.

### Stock cycle position (Step 1c)

**Classification: Recovery / Late-Cycle. Utilisation at peak; new capacity just starting.**

Revenue trajectory: FY23 ₹2,204 Cr → FY24 ₹2,376 Cr (+8%) → FY25 ₹2,524 Cr (+6%) → FY26 ₹2,496 Cr (−1%). Revenue growth has decelerated to flat — consistent with a company running at 96% utilisation with no additional capacity in the existing Punjab facilities. There is no organic volume growth left in the existing asset base.

Revenue decomposition: FY22–FY26 growth is primarily price-driven (realisation per spindle improved from ~₹3,600 in FY17 to ~₹6,700 currently). Volume growth has been bounded by spindle count (388,000 spindles, unchanged since ~FY23). This price-led growth is inherently fragile — it reverts when yarn-cotton spreads normalise.

Margin cycle: OPM recovered from 9% (FY24 trough) to 11–13% (FY25–FY26) as cotton prices softened. Q4 FY26 achieved 13.42% OPM — the best in recent quarters. However, this is still well below the FY21 peak of 16% and far below the anomalous FY22 peak of 28%. Margins are at mid-cycle recovery, not peak.

Capacity phase: **Beginning of heavy capex cycle.** ₹1,000 Cr Odisha greenfield (150,000 new spindles) has financial closure confirmed with construction started. Commercial operations expected Q3 FY27 (October–December 2026). This is the company's most significant growth investment — a 38% capacity increase.

Capex implications: OCF in FY25 was ₹415 Cr (strong, post-WC normalisation). With ₹1,000 Cr Odisha capex over FY27–FY28, and assuming OCF of ~₹350–400 Cr/year, incremental borrowing requirement is ~₹150–300 Cr. Total debt likely peaks at ₹600–750 Cr (from ₹463 Cr current) in FY27–FY28 — more manageable than the FY24 peak of ₹953 Cr, but still a leverage rebuild.

## 2. Business Quality

**Moat: NONE. Quality: MEDIUM (operationally competent, financially disciplined). Moat durability: N/A — never had one.**

Sportking is among the better-run yarn spinners in India. Its 388,000 spindles in Punjab run at 96% utilisation — genuinely best-in-class operational execution. Exports to 30+ countries, export share of ~52% of revenue, 25 MW rooftop solar (reducing energy costs), and merger with Marvel Dyers/Sobhagia Sales (vertical integration into fabric + garments) all signal a management team that is actively building a business beyond pure commodity spinning.

However, the honest assessment is that there is no durable competitive advantage in commodity yarn spinning:
- No pricing power over buyers (yarn is available from hundreds of mills globally)
- No pricing power over suppliers (cotton is a global commodity)
- Capital can and does replicate spindles cheaply; Sportking's cost position is good but not unique
- The FY22 ROCE of 53% was a COVID-era supply shock, not a moat being exercised — and the regression to 10–13% ROCE in FY23–FY26 confirms this

**Capital allocation**: The FY24–FY26 debt reduction (₹953 Cr → ₹463 Cr) on the back of strong OCF recovery is excellent. Promoter at 74.36% holding (direct, stable, no dilution). Dividend maintained at ₹1/share. The Odisha expansion is demand-linked and funded sensibly. However, heavy reliance on external debt for each capex cycle is a structural characteristic of the business.

**Customer concentration**: Not formally disclosed, but yarn is sold to hundreds of domestic and international fabric mills — no single customer concentration risk.

**Governance**: No SEBI notices, auditor qualifications, or promoter pledge found. Promoter holding has been stable at 74.36% for the last 3 years.

**10-year moat durability retrospective**: ROCE exceeded WACC (~14%) in only 2 of the 12 years on record — FY21 (21%) and FY22 (53%). In 10 of 12 years, ROCE was at or below WACC. This is definitively a no-moat business. The FY22 anomaly was a sector-wide event (pandemic supply shock driving yarn prices to generational highs) — it was not a Sportking-specific competitive advantage.

**What the 12-year dataset reveals:**

(1) *ROCE below WACC in 10 of 12 years.* Average ROCE including FY22 = 14.8% (barely above WACC). Excluding FY22 = 11.5% — firmly below the ~14% WACC for this risk class. There is no genuine moat. The business earns at or below its cost of capital in normal years.

(2) *FY22 was a generational anomaly.* OPM of 28% and PAT of ₹409 Cr in a ₹2,153 Cr revenue year reflects a global cotton and yarn supply shock that compressed supply while demand surged. This cannot be the base for any DCF assumption. The regression to 9–13% OPM in FY23–FY26 is the true structural margin range.

(3) *FY24 OCF crisis.* Negative OCF of −₹236 Cr against ₹70 Cr PAT = a ₹306 Cr working capital build. This was the company loading up on cotton inventory at elevated prices at the wrong point in the commodity cycle — a management misjudgment that cost significantly. The FY25 OCF recovery (₹415 Cr) confirms it was timing, not fraud, but it illustrates how volatile capital requirements are for this business.

(4) *Debt cycle behaviour.* Borrowings: ₹537 Cr (FY15) → ₹504 Cr (FY21, managed through cycle) → ₹953 Cr (FY24 peak, WC + capex) → ₹463 Cr (FY26, strong OCF repayment). The pattern is: build debt for capex/WC, generate OCF in good years to repay, repeat. The Odisha cycle will replay this: debt rises FY27, peaks FY28, then falls FY29+. Discipline is adequate but not exceptional.

(5) *Revenue CAGR reality.* FY15–FY26: 9% revenue CAGR. FY23–FY26: 4% CAGR (trough-to-flat recovery). Organic growth potential in existing capacity is exhausted at 96% utilisation. All future growth is contingent on Odisha commissioning.

## 4. Earnings Quality

**Classification: AGGRESSIVE BUT DEFENSIBLE**

- The FY22 OCF (₹96 Cr) vs PAT (₹409 Cr) divergence is the single biggest flag: ₹313 Cr of reported profit did not convert to cash. This was a WC build during the yarn boom — confirmed as real by FY23 OCF (₹520 Cr), which released the accumulated working capital. Cumulative 10-year OCF (₹1,659 Cr) significantly exceeds cumulative 10-year PAT (₹901 Cr) — proving earnings are real over the full cycle.
- FY24 negative OCF is attributable to cotton inventory timing (high prices, destocking in FY25).
- No promoter pledging. No auditor qualification. No SEBI notices found.
- Concern to monitor: Working capital intensity in the Odisha ramp-up phase (FY27–FY28). A repeat of the FY22/FY24 OCF-PAT divergence is likely during the high-utilisation ramp.

## 5. Valuation

### 5a. Price-Implied Expectations (Mauboussin)

At ₹177: EV = Market cap ₹2,255 Cr + Debt ₹463 Cr − Estimated cash ₹100 Cr = **₹2,618 Cr**

FY26 NOPAT: EBIT (PAT ₹120 Cr + Tax ~₹40 Cr + Interest ~₹40 Cr = ₹200 Cr EBIT) × 0.75 = **₹150 Cr**  
WACC = 7% (10-yr G-sec) + 6.5% × 1.10 (beta) = **14.15%**

PIE g = (EV × WACC − NOPAT) / (EV + NOPAT) = (2,618 × 0.1415 − 150) / (2,618 + 150) = (370.4 − 150) / 2,768 = **~8%**

| PIE Value Driver | Market-implied | Base-case view | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| NOPAT CAGR (perpetual) | ~8% | 5–6% organic + 1 Odisha step (FY27–FY29 only) | Negative — market is too optimistic on sustainable growth |
| NOPAT margin (steady-state) | ~6% (consistent with 11% OPM) | 6–7% (mid-cycle, achievable) | Neutral |
| Investment rate | HIGH (Odisha capex implied) | Very high FY27–FY28, then declining | Neutral |

**Expectations assessment**: The market is pricing 8% perpetual NOPAT growth — which requires Odisha to commission on time AND the UK/EU FTA export tail to be sustained AND margins to hold at current 11%+. Any one of these failing reverts growth to 4–5% (organic structural rate), which takes the stock 40–50% lower. The market is NOT pricing in a downside scenario. At only 8% implied growth, the market isn't egregiously bullish — but for a no-moat commodity business, there is zero embedded margin for error.

**Expectations treadmill**: For the stock to outperform at ₹177, NOPAT must sustain above 8% growth for more than 5 years — meaning Odisha must deliver + at least one follow-on expansion must be announced + export markets must recover meaningfully. Meeting the 8% target earns only CoC.

**Positive expectation triggers**:
1. Odisha commissioned on schedule (Oct 2026) and first revenue quarter (Q3/Q4 FY27) confirms volumes above 30% utilisation
2. US tariff normalisation to <15% restoring full export economics (major upside: ~₹50–70 Cr incremental PAT)
3. Cotton output stabilises / government supports MSP prices keeping input costs manageable

**Negative expectation triggers**:
1. Odisha construction delay beyond Q1 FY28 (eliminates FY27 revenue contribution narrative)
2. Cotton crop shortfall driving raw material prices above ₹8,000/quintal (erodes spreads to near zero)
3. Domestic yarn demand slowdown (both US tariffs AND domestic slowdown simultaneously squeezes both legs of the business)

**SVAR**: Bear scenario (cotton spikes, Odisha delayed 18 months, US tariffs remain, NOPAT growth ~2%):  
Downside EV = ₹150 Cr × 1.02 / (0.1415 − 0.02) = ₹1,260 Cr  
Downside equity = ₹1,260 − ₹363 net debt = ₹897 Cr → **₹70/share**  
SVAR = (₹177 − ₹70) / ₹177 = **60%** — asymmetry is UNFAVORABLE

The stock demonstrated this downside is real — it was trading at ₹78 as recently as its 52-week low. A 2× run from the bottom, in a sector facing structural headwinds, with heavy capex ahead, does not represent a balanced risk/reward.

## 8. Key Risks

1. **US tariff reinstatement / escalation**: ~49% of revenue is export. US tariffs peaked at 50% in FY26. If a new round of tariffs is imposed post FY26 trade resolution, the impact on yarn realization and order book would be immediate and severe. The company has no ability to pass through tariff costs.

2. **Cotton supply tightness (input cost spike)**: India's FY26–FY27 cotton crop is expected at a decadal low of 29.2 million bales. A supply shock driving cotton above ₹9,000/quintal would compress the cotton-to-yarn spread to near zero — directly causing an FY23–FY24 type earnings collapse.

3. **Odisha execution risk**: ₹1,000 Cr greenfield in Odisha — a state with different regulatory, labour, and infrastructure dynamics than Sportking's home base of Punjab. If commissioning slips beyond Q1 FY28, the growth thesis defers significantly and debt service pressure on the interim balance sheet intensifies.

4. **Working capital cycle risk**: As Odisha ramps, the company will need to finance yarn inventory for the new facility. A repeat of the FY24 OCF collapse (−₹236 Cr) is structurally likely in FY27–FY28. This could temporarily stress the balance sheet even if profitability holds.

5. **Valuation compression risk**: At 18.8× P/E, the stock is priced at the high end of its historical range AND above commodity sector peers (8–15× P/E). If the sector de-rates (as it did in FY23–FY24), the multiple can compress to 10–12× even with stable earnings — implying ₹95–115/share purely from multiple contraction with no fundamental deterioration.
